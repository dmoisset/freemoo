Chapter I - WarShip Construction

1.1 WarShips


The term "WarShip" refers to any armed, non-support military vessel used for exploration, expansion(conquest), and defense. Although interstellar empires often have gigantic fleets of support vessels -- trade ships, freighters, transports, communications probes, etc. -- it is often an empire's warfleet that draws it the most attention, and respect. And it is the warships that do the fighting in the cold depths of space. Naturally, it is these battles that roleplayers are most concerned with.
The nuts and bolts of ship combat will be covered in the next section. First, a prospective warrior must understand how a warship is put together and the statistics that describe it's structure.

1.2 Internal Structure: The Guts of a WarShip


A warship's "internal structure" is analogous to a biological creature's skeleton (assuming we're talking about endoskeletal creatures -- that is, creatures with internal skeletons). The internal structure is the framework that holds the ship's vital systems, and supports it against the forces exerted by rapid acceleration and complex manuvers. There are five "size classes" of ship. The larger the ship, the more internal structure required to support it and its vital systems. This also means larger ships can take more punishment from weapons before the internal framework is damaged to the point that the natural torques and accelerations of combat manuvers collapse the internal structure and rip the ship apart. This damage that a ship can take before breaking up is referred to as structural integrety (abbreviated SI for short). When a ship takes damage from a weapon strike, a certain number of points are subtracted from the ship's SI. When SI is reduced to 0 or less, the ship's structure has been damaged beyond its ability to withstand the stresses of spaceflight. At this point nothing can save the ship -- even if the ship wasn't executing some sort of combat manuver the torques and accelerations placed on the ship by the weapons hit are too much for the weakened frame, which is quickly torn apart. In short, when a ship loses all of its SI, it breaks apart and is destroyed.

1.3 Space and Critical Slots


The internal structure of a warship only allows so many weapons and systems to be installed before there is no more room. There are two quantities that are used to keep track of what has been placed on board a craft, and how much room is left for more: space and critical slots.
Space simply refers to the absolute amount of hull space available inside the ship for weapons and systems. Each weapon or system installed (obviously) requires a certain amount of space. This required space is subtraced from a ship's available space if the system is installed; when there is no more space left on the ship, nothing more can be installed. Of course if there is space left in the hull, but not enough for a prospective new system, that system cannot be installed.
Critical slots are similar to space; they are filled up as more systems are crammed into the hull. However, critical slots designate were on a ship a system has been placed. All ships have four facings: forward, rear, left, and right. When a ship is first designed, its total number of critical slots are distributed among these four facings. Systems placed in a critical slot in a certain facing are considered to be located in that facing. For example, a fusion beam placed in a forward critical slot is assumed to be on the forward side of the ship pointing into the (you guessed it) forward firing arc. Obviously the critical slots for the engine are predominantly in the rear of the ship. So, what are critical slots really used for? First of all, a weapon's location on a ship determines which firing arc it can target opponents in. Second, any internal damage done to a ship has a chance of damaging or destroying an internal system (the battle computer, the shield generator, the engine, a special system, or a weapon). Shots that come from the forward direction are most likely to damage systems in the forward part of the ship -- so any critical hits (damage to internal systems) are most likely to be to the systems in the forward part of the ship, which are represented by the forward critical slots.
There are a three rules regarding the distribution of critical slots on a ship. First, each facing must have at least one critical slot. Second, no facing can have more than 50% of a ship's total critical slots. Third, the left and right sides must have the same number of critical slots (to properly balance the ship). Other than this, the ship designer is free to assign a ship's critical slots as he or she sees fit. The number of criticals a device requires depends on the space it requires:

(number of criticals) = (space)/10

This value is rounded to the nearest whole number with only two exceptions. First, any special devices that each take up 5 or less space can share a critical slot with each other. Up to 14 space worth of specials can be assigned in this way. Second, like weapons may be assigned to critical slots as weapon pods. Up to 14 space worth of the weapon can be assigned in this manner to each critical slot. All weapons assigned to the same slot must be identical, and are controlled as a group: one roll is made when they are fired. The weapons either all hit the target or none of them hit the target!
Finally, in addition to the listed number of "free" critical slots (see Table 4-2), all ships have some engine criticals that represent the engine core. Half of the engine criticals must be located in the rear of the ship. The remaining slots can be placed in the rear, or distributed evenly on each side. In addition to the engine criticals, a ship using a battle computer has one additional critical slot devoted to it. This slot can be placed wherever the designer wishes.

1.4 Armor


All warships have armor: a thin layer of ultra-tough alloy that protects the ship's internal structure and systems (and crew) from high and low energy radiation (from stellar sources and also cosmic rays), micrometeoroids, and, of course, the weapons of other warships. The principle behind armor is simple: devise a substance that is thin, relatively lightweight, and promotes dissipation of kinetic energy and heat across its surface rather than through its surface and into the ship's internal support. In other words, a weapon strike on a ship's armor tends to blast off a section of the armor, rather than lance through layers of internal structure.
All ships have a certain base amount of armor. These armor points are distributed across a ship's four facings as the designer sees fit, with only three restrictions. First, every side must be given at least 10% of the total armor. Second, no side can have more than 50% of the total armor. Finally, the left and right sides of the ship must have equal amounts of armor.
Armor works much like SI. A weapon hit on a certain facing of a ship reduces that ship's armor points by a certain amount. Once the armor on that facing has been depleted (no more armor points left), any further strikes on that facing damage the ship's internal structure (SI) instead.
More advanced materials than the standard titanium can be used for armor, of course. These give a ship bonus armor points above its base number of armor points. This bonus is expressed as a percentage of the base. For example, Zortrium gives a bonus of +300%, so if a small ship has a base of 16 armor points, Zortium gives it 48 more armor points, for a total of 64!
Furthermore, since the materials used in armor are often used to strengthen and reinforce a ship's internal framework, advanced materials technology also adds bonus SI points using the same formula as the addition of bonus armor points. So, going back to the above example, a small ship has 8 SI. Zortium armor would give it 24 more, for a total of 36 SI.

1.5 Shields: The First Line of Defense

Deflector shields are powerful electromagnetic/gravitic energy fields that surround warships. Deflector shields function by taking the natural electrical charge inherant to all matter and, through rapid modulation, dispersing this electric charge over the local area of the "shield matrix" (the lines of force that describe the region of space where the shield exists). This causes beam weapons (even lasers, due to their electromagnetic nature) to lose coherance (and, thus, force) and "heavy matter" weapons (mass drivers and missiles) to be "deflected" violently away from the ship. In the case of missiles, this violent deflection often detonates the warheads; the force of the explosion is itself deflected away from the ship.
What makes a shield so effective is its localization: the region of projected electromagnetic force is so thin and dense that the acceleration it provides to objects trying to penetrate it is very extreme. To a certain extent, a shield can absorb force (i.e. damage) without any loss of this vital localization. This damage absorbed is rated as the shield's class. Excess damage is also deflected -- a shield lets nothing get through until its localization is too weak to effectively deflect anything -- but the excess kinetic energy forces the field to become less localized. Any deflector shield can only be "thinned" out to a certain critical level before the force absorption effect is too small to be of any use. Until then, however, the shield functions normally. Obviously, the amount of damage the shield can absorb outright is directly related to the amount of excess damage it can soak up before its localization smears out past the critical point -- the higher the shield's class the more excess damage it can absorb before the shield fails.
Larger, more powerful shield generators can produce denser fields, but not thicker fields. So while the amount of damage absorbed outright doesn't change, the shield can retain its effectiveness after being "spread out" more. In other words, the larger shield generators on larger ships can absorb more excess damage before failing.
Like armor, shields have four facings (frontal, rear, left, and right), each of which can absorb the given amount of damage before failing.

1.6 WarShip Engines


Hyperspace is the only known alternate universe that exists adjacent to every point in our universe. Although distances in hyperspace are identical to distances in our universe, certain aspects of this "alternate reality" make it prime for travel between the stars. Specifically, the speed of electromagnetic radiation in hyperspace is higher in hyperspace by orders of magnitude, and the effects of inertia are greatly reduced. The net result: even with a simple chemical rocket engine, a ship in hyperspace can reach an appreciable fraction of the speed of light in our universe. So more advanced engines, like the nuclear drive, or fusion engines, allow very efficient super- luminal speeds. Since all points in these universes coincide, this allows relatively short travel times between stars using rather conventional engines. It also means that, with the exception of a warp drive to push the ship into and out of hyperspace, a ship doesn't need a separate drive for travel in hyperspace and our universe.
A ship's engine, in game terms, really refers to the actual drive mechanism responsible for physically moving the ship (as opposed to the warp core responsible for pushing it into and out of hyperspace). The engines determine both a ship's FTL speed (for travel between stars) and its combat speed (for movement in combat). FTL speed is measured in parsecs/month. Combat speed is measured in movement points (MP). One MP will move a ship forward one hex on the combat map; Four MP will change its facing by one hexside.
Table 4-6 lists the base MP for a ship according to size class (larger ships are slower) and engine type (more advanced engines are faster). However the base MP can be increased by up to 10 if not all of the space on board the ship is used. In order to determine a ship's actual MP, divide remaining space by total space and multiply by 10 (you may need a calculator for this...). Round the result to the nearest whole number, and add it to the base MP. The result is the actual MP. For example, a small ship with a fusion drive has a base MP of 12. Pretend that the ship has 10 space used, which means it has 15 space free: 15 ö 25 = 0.6 10 = +6 MP. So the ship has an actual MP of 18.

1.8 Beam Weapons and Special Devices: The Good Stuff


With the details of the ship's basic construction and systems dealt with, the final topic in ship construction is all the goodies that can be added to a warship.
These "goodies" come in two major categories: weapons and special systems:
Weapons are devices designed to damage or destroy enemy targets: space stations, ground instillations, innocent populations, and, of course, enemy warships. Weapons come in a variety of sizes and types, each with a different set of capabilites.
Beam weapons are, as the name implies, beams of subatomic particles or coherant electromagnetic radiation that damage targets by superheating or vaporizing their constituent materials. Because beam weapons lose coherance with distance travelled, the damage they do is lessened as the range to the target becomes more remote.
Ballistic weapons behave much like beam weapons, but involve launching heavy pieces of matter at the target. They do damage through the sheer power of their kinetic energy -- they violently tear through the target. Ballistics do not lose coherance in the sense that beam weapons do: although the spread of the ordinance may increase very slightly with distance, each slug still has just as much kinetic energy as when it left the gunnel. Therefore, the damage done by ballistic weapons is not reduced with distance. However, ballistic weapons do require ammo -- which takes up extra space.
Missiles are guided, warhead-toting drones that deliver explosive ordinance to their targets. Thanks to their in-flight guidance systems, missiles always hit their target. This, of course, assumes that they are not destroyed by weapons fire or led astray by enemy electronic countermeasures. Oh. Although missiles have unlimited range and accuracy, they only have finite ammo supplies.
Torpedoes are "energy projectiles" -- globs of very energetic, unstable matter (or photons) in a magnetic confinement field. Upon striking the target, the confinement field ruptures. The resulting detonation damages the target. Like missiles torpedoes always hit unless they are led astray by electronic countermeasure systems. Luckily, torpedoes cannot be shot down. Sadly, they can only be fired every other turn.
Fighters are small, manned spacecraft typically armed with beam weapons or bombs. Fighters seek out enemy targets, fire at them from point blank range until running low on fuel, and then return to their mothership for refuel and reload.
Bombs are very high-yield explosive devices that can be dropped on enemy ground targets.
Special Devices are systems that somehow improve a ships performance, often in combat. They are too varied to go into in any detail here, however.

Chapter II - Combat

2.1 Ship-to-Ship Combat


When two hostile forces meet in space, the battle is played out on a hex grid (two standard BattleTech mats lined up end to end is a good playing field). Ships, missiles, torpedoes, and fighters are represented by tokens on the map (BattleSpace tokens will work just fine for this purpose, or you can make your own). Although alternate set ups are possible, typically each side sets up along a row on hexes on each side of the map.
If the battle is taking place near a planet, the planet must also be present on the map (usually on the defender's side, near the center of the width of his/her side of the map). The planet hexes cannot be moved into, although they can be passed over. The size of the planet on the map is determined by its size in the game (big shock!). Small and Tiny worlds take up only 1 hex. A Normal sized or Large world have a 2 hex radius (1 central hex, and all the adjacent hexes). Likewise, a Huge world has a 3 hex radius. It is often only necessary to mark the world's central hex on the map with a token of some sort; any other hexes occupied by the planet are then easily inferred from the planet's size.
The first step in a hostile engagement is to determine initiative. This is done individually for each ship using the following formula:

(Ship Beam Offense / 10) + Maximum MP

The result (rounded normally) is the ship's current initiative value. The ship with the highest value goes first, followed by the ship with the next highest value, etc. Two ships with identical values move and attack simultaneously.
Each ship, unless immobilized and disabled, gets a turn. During a ship's turn it can fire its weapons, use its special devices, and move as far as its MP will allow. Any weapons not fired can be saved for later in the combat round, but once a player states that a ship's turn is over, it cannot move again that round.

2.3 Firing Arcs


Before a ship can fire its beam or ballistic weapons, it must first determine which targets are in which weapon's firing arcs. If a target is out of a particular weapon's firing arc, that weapon cannot fire at that target.
All weapons mounted on the front of the ship (their criticals are in the front) fire into the front firing arc. The line of hexes extending out from the hex faces immediatly left and right of the ship's facing mark the boundaries of this firing arc.
Left mounted weapons can fire into the left firing arc. The line of hexes extending from the hexface to the left of the rear hexface (the hex face opposite the facing) and the line of hexes extending from the facing hexface mark the boundaries of the left firing arc.
Right mounted weapons can fire into the right firing arc. The line of hexes extending from the hexface to the right of the rear hexface and the line of hexes extending from the facing hexface mark the boundaries of the right firing arc. The rear mounted weapons fire into the (surprise!) rear firing arc. The line of hexes extending from the hexfaces to the left and right of the rear hexface are the boundaries of the rear firing arc.
Missiles, torpedoes, fighters, and bombs do not have or use firing arcs. When missiles, torpedoes, and fighters are launched, they launch into the hex in the direction of their target. Bombs require the ship to be adjacent to the planet hexes (i.e. bombs have a range of 1).
Also, a target in the same hex as the attacker in considered to be in all of the attacker's firing arcs.

2.5 Firing Missiles and Torpedoes

A missile or torpedoes is initially launched into the hexface in the direction of its target. All missiles and torpedoes automatically get initiative at the beginning of the next combat round.
A missile has a movement rate of 12 spaces, +2 spaces for every drive type researched beyond Nuclear. A torpedo has a movement rate of 24.
When the missile or torpedo reaches the hex its target is in, the attacker calculates the chance to hit. Before the roll is made, however, the defender may fire any point defense weapons he or she has available at the missiles (range 0). A missile's defense rating is 40 + (5 per movement rate). So, for example, a missile using a Fusion Drive (Movement rate of 14 spaces) would have a defense rating of 40 + 70 = +110. The amount of damage a missile can take without being destroyed depends on the type of missile, and is given in the tables at the end of the combat section. Torpedoes can be jammed, but they cannot be shot down. The chance of a missile or torpedo hitting its target is given by the following formula:

100% + Scanner Bonus - Enemy Jammer = % Chance to Hit

The attacker rolls d100. If the result is equal to or below the % Chance to Hit, the missile or torpedo strikes the target and does damage.

2.6 Fighters

When launched at a target, each fighter squadron (4 fighters) enters the hexface in the direction to the target. Like missiles and torps, fighters always get initiative at the beginning of the next combat turn. A fighter's movement rate depends on the type of fighter, but the squadrons will always take the fastest, most direct route to the target.
Upon arriving in the target's hex, as with missiles, the defender may fire his or her point defense weapons at the fighters. The range is 0, and a fighter's defense rating is calculated in the same way as a missile's. A fighter has a base defense rating of 40.
Once any point defense is taken care of, each fighter squadron fires at the target. Interceptors carry the best point defense weapon available. Bombers carry the best bomb available. Heavy fighters carry two of the best point defense beams and two of the best bombs. While bombs automatically hit the target, a squadron makes a single roll to see if all of its beams hit or miss. The chance of a beam weapon hitting is given by the following formula:

(Beam Offense + 45) - Target Beam Defense = % Hit

The attack itself is rolled once for each squad, and success is determined in the same manner as for regular beam weapons. If the squad's attack hits, all of the beam weapons on the fighters in the squad hit. Attacks are always against the ship's weakest shield facing. If all of the ship's facings have the same shield strength, the attacker chooses which facing the fighters attack.
For example, a group of 12 interceptors (3 squads) with Optronic computers (+50% Beam Offense) and point defense fusion beams (3 damage) target an enemy cruiser with Class I Shields. The target's Beam Defense is 70. 50 + 45 - 70 = +25, so the hit is automatic. The damage done is thus (3 - 1) * 4 = 8. Since all the shield facings are at full, the attacker chooses to target the rear shield facing. The shield absorbs 8 damage. The second squad also does 8 damage. Two points are absorbed by the rear shield before it collapses. The remaining 6 are absorbed by the ship's armor. The final squad fires, again at the rear facing of the ship. This time, with the shield down, the damage is 3 * 4 = 12 damage. Three points are absorbed by the ship's armor (the ship had 9 points of armor on the rear facing). The other 9 points are done to the ship's structural integrety.

2.7 Bombing


Bombs are used to attack ground targets, and can also be loaded onto Bombers and Heavy Fighters for attacking other ships. Bombs always hit their target -- they cannot be jammed, shot down, or deflected. Shields and armor, however, do block damage normally.
When loaded onto a ship, bombs have a range of 1 unless the ship is equipped with transporters. In the latter case, the bombs have a range of 12 spaces. In either case, bombs loaded onto a WarShip cannot be used to attack other WarShips or any orbital target.

2.8 Doing Damage

Obviously, when a weapon hits its target, it does damage. What the damage is done to, and how to handle it, is the subject of this section.
Damage is first done to the ship's shields, specifically a certain number of points of damage are subtracted from each individual attack. The balance is then applied to the shield's damage absorption capabilities. With the exception of enveloping weapons (described in the next section), the damage is done to whatever facing the weapon strikes. This is determined by "drawing" (you don't really need to draw a line on the map -- just use a ruler or some other straightedge) a straight line from the center of the attacker's hex to the center of the defender's hex. The hexside this line passes through determines which table to roll on to determine what facing to apply the damage to: since space is really three dimensional, just because an attack comes from the front of a ship doesn't mean it hits the front section. If the hexside the attack passes through to reach the defender is the hexside the defender is facing, or the one to either side, the damage location is rolled on the front facing table. If the attack passes through the left rear hexside adjacent to the left "front" hexside, the damage location is rolled on the left facing table. If the attack passes through the right rear hexside adjacent to the right "front" hexside, the damage location is rolled on the right facing table. Finally, if the attack passes through the rear hexface (the hexface opposite the front hexface), the damage location is rolled on the rear facing table.

2.9 Marines

Each size class of ship has a standard complement of marines as crew. These marines are basically responsible for running the ship, and include pilots, navigators, gunners, engineers, communicators, and doctors. Many are trained in multiple jobs, so if one is killed, another can take over until a replacement is found.
Hits by enemy weapons can kill marines. Every five points of hard radiation damage that gets past the shields kills a marine. Furthermore, every point of normal damage inflicted in a round has a 1% chance of killing a marine. If more than 100 damage was inflicted, one marine is automatically killed, and the rest of the damage is used to calculate the chance another is killed. If more than 200 damage is inflicted, two marines are automatically killed, and the rest of the damage is used to calculate the change another is killed ... and so on.
Shipboard marines are also used in boarding actions against other ships -- raids and attempts to capture the enemy ship. Because of this, all of a ship's marines are typically armed with the best available gear.
Without assult shuttles or transports, enemy marines can only attempt to board an enemy ship that is immobile (unable to move). The attacking ship must no more than 1 hex away from the target ship, and a certain minimum complement must remain on board the attacking ship if it is remain functional.
If the attacker has transporters, a boarding action can be attempted whenever the defender is 12 or fewer hexes away and the shield facing in the direction of the attacker is down. A damper field will block a transport attempt -- all transporting marines will be automatically killed.
Assult shuttles automatically drop off their marines upon arriving at the enemy ship (after the enemy ship has had a chance to fire its point defense weapons). Assult shuttles can only be used for capture atttempts, however. Raids are not permitted (the marines aren't that suicidal).
Marines can be given two missions: capture or raid. A capture attempts to gain control of the enemy ship. A raid attempts to destroy systems on board the enemy ship.
To capture a ship, the attacking marines must kill or incapacitate the entire opposing crew. To conduct a successful raid, they need only survive a round of crew combat.
In order to conduct marine combat each side rolls a d100 and adds the appropriate combat bonuses for their troops. This number is then multiplied by the number of troops in the side. The winner is the side with the highest number -- casualties are figured as follows:

Every 50 points corresponds to 1 hit of damage. The winner adds 1 hit for every 50 points he has more than the loser. The loser subtracts 1 hit for every 50 points he has less than the winner. Each side divides the number of hits inflicted by the number of hits each marine has. The result is the number of casualties for that side. What happens next depends on the purpose of the boarding action.
If the attacker's intent was capture, and the entire defending crew was killed, the attacker has captured the enemy ship. If there are still attackers and defenders, both ships may still act, and the next combat round (at the beginning of the round) there will be another round of fighting. It is also possible for the attacking marines to retreat back to their vessel if they are able -- they need transporters, assult shuttles, or their vessel must be no more than 1 hex away. If all of the attackers are killed, the defender (obviously) wins, and retains control of the ship. If both the attackers and defenders have all been killed, the ship becomes a derelict. The winner of the fight can pick it up after the combat, or someone can attempt to board and hold it.
A captured ship can only be immediately controlled by a telepathic race. Such a race can mind-control some of the defending marines into running the ship. If the race isn't telepathic, the victorious marines will need some time to figure out how to work the ship; they will not be able to use the ship until the combat is over. If the side that captured the ship loses the combat or retreats, the captured ship reverts to the origional owner unless the marines on board were telepathic and made the ship retreat. If the original owner recaptures the ship during that combat, he/she may immediately take control of it. If the original owner recaptures the ship in a later combat, he too must wait until the end of the battle to be able to use the ship (the captors needed to make a few adjustments to run the ship, so it's controls are no longer quite familiar to the original race) ... unless, of course, the original owner is also telepathic.
If the attacker's intent was a raid, any marines that survive the combat attack the ship's internal systems. Roll 1d100 again and add the attacker's bonuses. Multiply again by the number of attacking marines left. Every 50 points is 1 automatic critical hit against the enemy ship -- randomly determined. If the criticals destroy the engine, the attacking marines are killed as the ship explodes unless they have transporters. In the latter case, they simply transport back to their ship. Otherwise, a raid always ends with the attacking marines returning to their vessel. One very important note: one defending marine will always survive the raid unless the ship is destroyed. This is because at least 1 defending marine was piloting the ship instead of fighting during the raid.

Chapter III - Weapons and Special Systems

This section covers, in detail, the weapons and special systems that can be placed onboard a warship.

3.1 General Considerations

All weapons and systems require a certain amount of space and critical slots. The latter is related to the former: to get the number of critical slots needed for a weapon or system, divide the space required for the system by 10 and round normally.

Furthermore, weapons that take up less than 10 space can be combined into weapons pods. Weapons pods simulaneously aim and fire a group of weapons at a single target. All of the weapons must be of the same type, with the same modifications, and only one attack roll is made. If the roll hits, all of the weapons hit. If it misses, all of the weapons miss. Finally, since the pod takes up only one critical space, a hit to that critical space destroys all the weapons in the pod. The maximum amount of space in a single weapons pod is 14.
For example, a fully miniturized laser with no modifications requires 2.5 space. Five lasers together would require 12.5 space (which rounds up to 13). So a weapons pod with five of these lasers would require 1 critical slot and 13 space. Special systems can also be crammed into single critical slots. Each system must take up 5 space or less, and the total amount of space can be no greater than 14. Like weapons pods, a critical to that slot destroys all of the systems in the slot.

All weapons and many special systems can be miniturized. Miniturization reduces the size and cost of these systems, and is based on the number of fields researched beyond the weapon's own field.

3.2 Beam Weapons

Beam weapons fire focused, coherant beams of photons, subatomic particles, or plasma. The beam usually does damage with its incredable heat and highly focused impact point. All beam weapons have a problem with range dissipation -- the tendency for beams to lose coherance over distance. This causes beam weapons to do less damage the further away the target is. Also, distant targets are harder to hit, so beam weapons have a to-hit penalty with range.

There are several modifications that can be applied to beam weapons that affect their performance.

Heavy Mount weapons are larger, more powerful versions of normal beam weapons. They produce a more energetic, more coherant beam, although their large size makes it impossible to target missiles and fighters. A Heavy Mount weapon has twice the range of a normal weapon, a 50% bonus to damage, and suffers half the normal damage penalties with range. The weapon requires twice the space and cost of a normal weapon. The Heavy Mount and Point Defense modifications are mutually exclusive.

Point Defense weapons are smaller, more accurate versions of normal beam weapons used to target incoming missiles and fighters. Point Defense weapons do only 50% the normal amount of damage, and have double the range penalties for damage. They, however, are more precise, and have +25% to their to-hit percentage. Point Defense weapons are 50% the size and cost of a normal weapon. They require the intended weapon to have undergone 1 level of miniturization, and are exclusive with Heavy Mount weapons.

Continuous Fire weapons are able to maintain their beam longer without overheating. This improves their accuracy by +25%, and increases their damage by 10%. The extra space required for advanced focusing equipment and heat controls increase the size and cost of the weapon by 50%. The modification requires the intended weapon to have undergone 1 level of miniturization.

Pulse Fire weapons have rapidly recharging capacitors and more advanced heat control systems. As a result, a pulse weapon fires three rapid, full-strength shots rather than one. Because the battle computer doesn't have as long to calculate its target's position and movement, there is a -20% penalty to accuracy. Furthermore, there is no time to adjust the weapon position between shots -- they either all hit or miss, so only one attack roll is required. This modification requires the intended weapon to have undergone 2 levels of miniturization and adds 50% to the weapon's size and cost.

Armor Piercing weapons use powerful focusing elements to greatly confine the beam width. This narrow beam is barely dissipated by armor, so the beam cuts through as if the armor wasn't there and does damage directly to the ship's SI. Of coure, the beam is still vulnerable to deflector shields, and even armor piercing beams cannot penetrate Xentronium or any Heavy Armor. This modification requires 1 level of miniturization, and increases the size and cost of the weapon by 50%.

Shield Piercing weapons use charge masking, phase variations, or both to make the beam "invisible" to a target's shields. These weapons ignore a target's shield completely, unless the target has a Hard Shield installed. This modification requires 1 level of miniturization, and increases the size and cost of the weapon by 50%.

Enveloping beam weapons surround the target on impact and inflict their damage on all four facings. Any damage from any facing that penetrates the armor does damage to the SI of the ship. Enveloping increases the size and cost of a weapon by 100%, and require the intended weapon to have undergone 2 levels of miniturization.

No Range Dissipation weapons use advanced focusers and power boosters to eliminate a beam's damage penalty with range. This modification increases the size and cost of the weapon by 25%, and requires the intended weapon to have undergone 1 level of miniturization.

Hard Radiation beams are deadly to living tissue. Every 5 points of damage that penetrates the target's shields kills one marine on board the ship. Only a Damper Field can block this effect.

Electromagnetic Pulse generate powerful bursts of electromagnetic energy. These weapons damage shields normally, but ignore a ship's armor. Every 5 damage they do to internal structure results in an automatic critical hit as the lethal blasts of electromagnetic energy fry internal systems.

Gravitic weapons generate enormously powerful tidal forces in the interior of the ship. This shearing force results in extra internal damage: a strike to the ship's armor also does 50% damage directly to the ship's SI (no chance of criticals). Any damage that penetrates the armor does double damage to the ship's structure. For example, two gravitic beams strike a ship with 16 armor points for 10 damage each. The first beam does 10 damage to the armor and 5 to the SI. The second beam does 6 damage to the armor and 11 to the SI (6ö2 + 4 2 = 11).

Double Range Dissipation weapons lose conherance quickly with distance. As a result, these weapons have double the normal range penalties with distance.

Automatic Hit weapons have such a wide field of effect that there is no need to roll for a hit -- the weapon always strikes its target.

Planet Busters are weapons capable of generating a burst of energy powerful enough to reduce a world to an asteroid belt!

Laser Cannons are the most basic beam weapons. Laser is an acronym for light amplification by stimulated emission of radiation. A laser beam is a stream of coherant, same-wavelength photons. The beam can be tightly focused and easily modified and amplified, allowing for pulse fire, continuous fire, armor piercing, and no range dissipation.

Fusion Beams fire focused beams of superheated fusion plasma. Fusion beams have low coherance, and as such have double the range penalties for damage. Added heat sinks allow for continuous fire, and, like all plasma weapons, fusion beams can be made enveloping.

Neutron Beams, in spite of the name, fire focused beams of both neutrons and alpha particles. The latter are used in the reaction to create and accelerate the neutrons -- the chargeless neutrons are very difficult to focus and accelerate alone. Added heat sinks allow for continuous fire, and neutron radiation is a very penetrating form of hard radiation.

Ion Cannons fire focused pulses of alternating protons and electrons that generate an extremely powerful current across the beam and in the target. The currents created by this weapon cascade through the target's armor, damaging both internal systems and internal structure. Ion Cannons are large and unweildy weapons -- they can be given the continuous fire modification with some localized heat dissipation equipment, but they are too big to make into heavy mount weapons: the resulting syncotron radiation would be lethal to the ship's crew. Likewise, they cannot be made into point defense weapons since that would reduce the particle accelerator's size too much.

Graviton Beams fire narrow a narrow, intense stream of gravitons that set up a tremendous shearing force across their target. This shearing force has a devistating effect on a ship's structural integrety. Any damage to the armor does 50% damage to the structure as well (no chance of critical). Any damage directly to structure is doubled (with chance of critical). Graviton beams can be built as a large platform weapon, but the difficulty inherant in creating intense graviton fields (gravitons are, individually, extreme weak force carriers) makes point defense graviton beams ineffective.

Phasors fire intense, coherant streams of visible, UV, and X-ray radiation simultaneously in normal and hyperspace. Because they are similar, in principle, to a laser, phasors can be modified as point defense, heavy mount, continuous, and pulse weapons. Their "multi-phased" component however, allows them to skirt a target's shields with only a few modifications. However, the difficulty of increasing coherance simultaneously in normal and hyperspace rules out the No Range Dissipation mod.

Plasma Cannons are devistating, high-yeild plasma weapons that utilize a combination of fusion and matter-antimatter reactions to generate a powerful and naturally enveloping blast of superheated plasma. Like fusion beams, plasma cannons can be made continuous. While it is rather easy to scale them up to a heavy mount, the bulkiness of all the required heat dissipation equipment makes a point defense version very impractical. And, like all plasma weapons, plasma cannons have severe coherance problems that double the range penalties to damage.

Mauler Devices are tremendous, brute-force energy cannons with a very wide spread. A mauler blast always strikes its target, but the damage dissipation due to range is doubled -- a tradeoff for the wide spread attribute. Maulers can be scaled up to a heavy platform, but the energy generators required are simply too large to allow for point defense modifications.

The Stellar Converter is the pinnacle in plasma weapon research. This awesome cannon uses a city block's worth of plasma collectors, fusion generators, capacitors, and heat dissipators to launch a ship-sized stream of super-dense plasma heated to ten billion degrees Celsius! The devistating blast envelops the target, automatically doing 400 damage to each facing! The density of the plasma stream precludes dissipation with range as well.
Furthermore, a Stellar Converter can, over the course of several hours (during which the ship cannot engage in any activities save for minor orbital corrections and generating nominal life support) build its plasma banks up to a million times normal density and a thousand times normal temperature. Although the weapon must be fired immediately, the focused plasma stream can melt a planet's crust and mantle, and set up a shockwave in the core that rips the world apart! A few hours after the planet's destruction, the debris will cool and coelesce into an asteroid field.

Death Rays are legendary weapons reputed to have once been developed by the Orions. Although no known extant race in the galaxy has replicated this technology, there are plenty of records about them. Death rays strike their target with the force of a Mauler device, and the radiation they carry is quite lethal to living tissue. Legend has it that a single strike by a death ray could leave a ship a drifting derelict -- the ship may survive, but the radiation would instantly kill the crew!

Particle Beams are another legendary weapon of the Orions. Smaller than death rays, particle beams were reputed to have been naturally shield piercing. Little else is known about them.

3.3 Ballistic Weapons

Ballistic weapons fire slugs, shells, and other projectiles at their target. They rely on the kinetic energy of their ordinance to damage the target. Because kinetic energy is not dissipated as the slugs travel through space, ballistic weapons do not suffer a range-based penalty to damage. They do, however, suffer a to-hit penalty based on range, just like beam weapons.
Ballistic weapons also differ from beam weapons in that they require ammunition. Once a ballistic weapon runs out of ammo, it cannot be fired until it has been reloaded (usually at a friendly colony or star base). The ammo number listed in the table is the number of shots the weapon emplacement itself holds. Every 10 space (1 critical) allocated to extra armor increases a weapon's available ammo by twice the amount listed under ammo.

Heavy Mount ballistic weapons are large platform versions of normal ballistic weapons. The increased size and speed of the projectile boosts the weapon's damage by 50% and gives it a maximum range of 48 spaces. Heavy mount increases the size and cost of a weapon by 100%.

Point Defense weapons are small, precise copies of the normal weapon often used to target incoming fighters and missiles. Point defense ballistic weapons do half normal damage and have a maximum range of 12. They are also half the size and cost of normal ballistic weapons. Point defense requires

Also, since all of the slugs are fired without a chance to adjust the weapon's aim, a single roll is all that is required: a miss indicates all three shots miss -- a hit means all three shots hit. Naturally, firing the weapon uses three rounds of ammo. Autofire weapons have a 50% increase in size and cost, and autofire isn't available until the weapon has undergone two levels of miniturization.

Armor Piercing weapons use particularly fast and heavy shells to rip through a target ship's armor. An armor piercing weapon penetrates all forms of armor except for Xenotronium or Heavy Armor. The result is a tight, cohesive region of much higher than normal energy that travels at the speed of light toward its target. Upon striking the target, the zero-point energy surge is converted directly into kinetic energy, often explosively vaporizing anything it hits. While the region of energy "disruption" is itself totally invisible, one interesting side effect is an extreme "blue-shifting" of the natural microwave background photons to the green part of the spectrum. This generates a brilliant green glow in the region of energy disruption, acting as a natural tracer for the disruptor blast!
Disruptors are very complex weapons. As such, they cannot be scaled down enough for a point defense platform, although they can most certainly be scaled up to heavy mount. A disruptor can also be equipped with a rapidly recharging energy inducer, allowing the weapon to be fired in an "autofire" mode.
Although in some respects a disruptor is much like a beam weapon, the total lack of range dissipation and the fact that it is (technically) kinetic energy that is used to destroy the target leads to its classification as a ballistic weapon. In any case, a disruptor doesn't use or need ammo.
The Annihilator is another legendary weapon developed by the Orions. This powerful weapon's "ammo" was nothing less than a miniature black hole! This "quantum singularity" was generated in the weapon's vaccuum chamber, and accelerated at close to the speed of light out of the gunnel. The gravitational riptides created by this awesome weapon were augmented by the short lifetime of the black hole. The hole would return its "formation energy" to the cosmos within a second, generating an explosion in the process.

3.4 Missiles and Torpedoes

Missiles are guided drones that carry a high-explosive warhead to their target. Torpedoes use magnetic containment fields to hold volatile materials. The containment field ruptures when the torpedo strikes its target, causing an explosion.
Unless they are jammed, both missiles and torps always hit their target. They, typically, suffer no reduction to their damage from range.
The standard emplacement of a missile has enough ammo to fire the missile five times. Every ten additional shots requires 10 space and 1 critical to be allocated for the missiles. Ammo for missiles never miniturizes . . . although the missile emplacement itself does. Missiles can be targeted by point defense and normal-sized weapons. Each missile requires a certain number of "hits" to be inflicted on it before it is destroyed. Also, a single beam blast can destroy no more than one missile per shot. The one exception is the Mauler device. Maulers, by virtue of the fact that they produce a very wide angle blast, can damage every missile in the target hex. A normal-sized Mauler destroys (Mauler damage/Missile "hits") missiles.
Torpedoes do not require ammo; they require one full turn to recharge before they can fire again. Also, torps cannot be shot down, they can only be jammed.
The following modifications can be applied to missiles and torpedoes:

Multiple Independantly targetable Re-Entry Vehicle missiles each carry four full-strength warheads that separate from the delivery vehicle just before impact. These warheads can be targeted to either all hit the same facing, or each to hit one facing. Note, however, that the warheads do not have to be targeted invididually: if the missile is jammed or destroyed, all of the warheads are lost. This modification increases the size and cost of the missiles by 100%, and is only possible after the missiles have undergone 2 levels of miniturization.

Electronic Counter Countermeasures are electrical targeting and retargeting systems that aid a missile or torpedo in retaining its lock on a target that is attempting to jam its guidance computer. ECCM projectiles halve the effect of the target's jamming device. They add 25% to the size and cost of the projectile, and require it to have undergone 1 level of miniturization.

Heavily Armored missiles use the race's best researched armor to protect the warheads, greatly increasing the amount of damage needed to destroy the projectile. The bonus to the missile's "hits" are equal to the bonus normally provided by the armor. For example, a Nuclear Missile (4 hits) armored with Zortrium (+12 hits) will withstand 16 damage. This modification adds 25% to the size and cost of the missile, and requires the missile to have undergone 1 level of miniturization.

Fast missiles and torpedoes have a +8 bonus to their combat speed. This also adds 40 to a missile's defense rating against defensive weapons. The fast modification adds 25% to the projectile's size and cost, and requires 1 level of miniturization.

Emissions Guidance missiles track the waste "trail" produced by a ship's engines in combat and follow it right up the target's tailpipe. This can have devistating effects if the missile hits: the missile automatically ignores the target's armor, there is a +2 to the critical hit roll (normal damage modifiers apply), and all critical hits are to the ship's engines. EMG missiles are only available to races with EMG technology. Because the mod requires a very advanced guidance, targeting, and manuvering system for the missile, it increases the size and cost of the missile by 4 times.

Overloaded torpedoes use a more powerful containment field to hold a much larger explosive payload. Even then, the payload almost always exceeds their engineering safety limits, requiring extra shielding and fail-safe equipment in the loaders and torpedo tubes. As a result, overloaded increases the damage done by a torpedo by 50%, but increases the size and cost by 25%. The torpedo must have undergone 2 levels of miniturization for this modification to be applicable.

Enveloping torpedoes surround their target on impact, doing their damage to all four of the target's facings. Any damage from any facing that penetrates the armor is inflicted to the SI normally. The enveloping modification adds 100% to the torpedo's size and cost, and requires that the weapon have undergone 2 levels of miniturization.

No Range Dissipation strengthens the stability of a plasma torpedo enough so that it doesn't lose strength as it travels. This mod increases the size and cost of the weapon by 25%, and requires 1 level of miniturization.

Fighters lack hyperspacial capabilities, but their small size makes them extremely quick and manuverable. Fighters are rearmed and repaired in the hangers of larger WarShips, where they are launched at specific targets. Fighters are hangered and launched in squadrons of 4, and the fighters in a squad move and attack in unison.
Fighters do not miniturize, although a race with a tech level higher than the fighter's tech level may be able to modify the fighters somewhat. 3.6 Bombs and Biological Weapons

Bombs and biologicals represent a very powerful class of weapons designed to target colonies and other ground instillations. Bombs and bio weapons tend to be very light and compact, and yet have tremendous destructive potential. The tradeoff is accuracy; bomb-type weapons do not carry any form of guidance or targeting capability, so they are only effective against very large targets where collateral damage is important.
Each bomb/bioweapon emplacement in the ship holds 10 bombs, and has one drop chute. Bombs, like missiles and ballistics, require ammo. Extra ammo for a specific bomb chute is allocated in 10 space increments. The specific amount of ammo depends on the bomb.
Bombs have a maximum range of 1 space, and automatically hit their target, doing a random amount of damage (the "damage" done to a colony depends on the strategic value of where the bomb actually hits). Bio weapons are a similar ball game, but their method of damage is described below.
A transporter system can greatly facilitate the delivery of bombs or bio weapons to a planetary target. A transporter- equipped ship can deliver a bomb or biological weapon payload to a planetary target from up to 12 hexes away!
Biological weapons deserve special mention because of their method for doing damage. Each biological weapon dropped on a world automatically kills a certain percentage of the population, listed under damage. Multiple drops are figured as follows:
With each drop, there is less population to kill, and a greater number that will be resistant to the bio weapon. Each drop, however, does have a minimum number of population per drop that can die -- so it is possible to totally kill of a population with a bio weapon.
Use the following formula to calculate the number of survivors after x number of drops:

No. survivors = Total Population [1 - (% Killed ö 100)] ^ No. Drops
Min No. Killed = Min strength number of drops

The actual number killed is Total Population - No. survivors. If this is less than Min No. Killed, then use Min No. Killed as the number of population killed and determine the casualties from the attack.

For example, a race uses death spores (2% Killed) on a colony with a population of 11 million. Death Spores have a Min No. Killed = 80,000 people/drop. During the course of the fight, the race drops 20 death spores on their target:

No. survivors = 11 million [.98] ^ 20 = 7.3 million
Min No. Killed = 80 thousand 20 = 2.4 million

The actual number killed was (11 million - 7.3 million = ) 3.7 million people, which is higher than 2.4 million, so the Min No. Killed is ignored.

Bombs and biological weapons do not have, need, or use modifications.

Bio Terminators are one of the most hideous organisms ever developed. Based on the study of a particularly deadly, but non- sentient, alien life form first encountered by the Meklar, the Bio-Terminator is really a kind of "dual-species". An adult "Bio-Terminator" is an eight foot long, heavily-armored, insectiod killing machine with long, powerful claws and a whip- like, segmented tail. However, the embryo for this adult form is carried by a parasitic organism that resembles a cross between a crab and a spider. This organism is commonly referred to as the "larval" stage, and a DNA analysis would confirm that the larval stage and the adult are simply two totally distinct expressions of the Terminator's compete genetic code.
The "larval" stage is nocturnal, and very adept at ambushing isolated prey. The larva attaches to the face of its victim, providing oxygen and nutrients to its host, but inducing coma and snaking an ovipositor down the victim's throat. When the ovipositor reaches the victim's chest region, it deposits an embryo of the "adult" form, that is then fed by the larva. This process totally depletes the larval form of energy -- it separates from its victim after about twelve hours, crawls off and dies. The victim remains in a coma for about one hour before returning to consciousness with no memory of anything that has happened for the past twenty-four to fourty-eight hours.
In the mean time, the embryo rapidly grows inside its victim for about twelve more hours. It then tears its way out of the victim's chest, killing the victim and providing the new "adult" bio-terminator with it's first meal. This provides the energy needed to, in less than twelve hours, grow to full adulthood.
The fully-grown bio terminator hunts, kills and eats indescriminantly to provide the energy it needs to lay the tough, leathery eggs that birth the larva form. It will also attempt to capture and cacoon some victims near its eggs to allow the larvae easy access to more hosts. A single bio terminator in a populated area can hunt and kill up to 40 people in one day -- with each kill providing the energy it needs to lay one egg. Also, unlike an infection, a bio terminator can easily seek out a new location if its food source becomes scarce.
A single drop of these creatures, consisting of seven storage canisters with the larval form inside, will kill 4% of the population, or at least 150,000 people, before the infestation can be brought under control. Multiple drops are not as effective percentage wise, although each drop will still kill a minimum of 150,000 people.

The compressor causes 1-8 4 damage per size class of the target ship to all ships within 6 hexes, and this damage is done directly to the ship's SI (with normal chances of a critical hit). The damage to missiles and fighters is 1-8 2, and the compressor ignores a missile's heavy armor and a fighter's shields.

The next turn, the wake builds up strength until, at the beginning of the second turn after its creation, it triggers the total collapse of the space time continuum toward the center of the spherical wake. In other words, the wake collapses into a black hole around the hapless ship imprisoned inside! This, of course, destroys the ship. There is nothing that can save a ship once it is confined in the gravitic wake -- even subspace teleporters are neutralized by this device!

ATCR Loaders are powerful field boosters and auxillary capacitors for the ship's torpedo launchers (ATCR, pronounced 'At-Ser', stands for Accelerated Torpedo Capacitor Recharge). This system allows a ship to fire its torps every turn rather than every other turn.

Achilles Targeting Units quick scan and analyze enemy ship data to determine weak points in the ship's armor and calculate strategic firing points that make critical hits to vital internal systems more likely. This powerful analysis tool allows all onboard beam and ballistic weapons to ignore the target's armor, and increases the chance of a critical hit by +4.

Augmented Engines are auxillary manuvering jets and engine power boosters designed to give the ship a bonus to its combat speed and manuverability. Ships with this system gain a +5 to combat speed and a +25 to ship defense. Augmented Engine criticals must be placed in the rear or side slots of the ship.

Automated Repair Units are computer controlled drones and damage control centers interfaced with the intention of quickly repairing damaged onboard systems, reinforcing weakened structure, and patching holes in the ship's armor. A ship equipped with a repair unit can restore an amount of damage equal to 10% of the ship's total armor and SI each turn. It will also restore one system damaged by a critical hit each turn (player's choice). Damaged repaired to armor is applied evenly to all facings when possible.

Battle Pods are dedicated bays designed to add 50% to a ship's total available space. A number of critical slots equal to the extra space divided by 10 (round normally) are also added, and may be place wherever the designer wants. Battle Pod space can be filled with additional weapons and systems, but the addition of mass invariably slows down the ship: Every 20% of the battle pod that is filled reduces the ship's combat speed by 1, for a maximum penalty of -5.

Battle Scanners are dedicated targeting systems that increase the ship offense by +50.

Cloaking Devices create an optical (actually, it's a broad spectrum) distortion field that makes the ship invisible to most scanners. A cloaked ship has a +80 bonus to ship defense. The capabilities. While uncloaked, the ship loses its defensive bonus. The ship may recloak on its next turn, but only if it doesn't fire any weapons.

Damper Fields are a legendary, and impressive, technology developed by the ancient Orions. These devices create a field around the ship that gravely lowers the value of Planck's constant, sapping anything passing through of much of its kinetic and radient energy. Furthermore, thanks to the nature of quantum effects, the field can be switched off just long enough to fire weapons and then restored to full strength immediately. The net effects of this are impressive indeed: 75% of all damage inflicted on the ship is simply absorbed by the field. Furthermore, transporter beams and hard radiation cannot penetrate the field at all. The down side is that the field makes shields equally useless -- a ship equipped with a damper field simply cannot have a functional shield operating at the same time, including a hard shield. The technology to build these devices has been lost since the time of the Orion Empire.

Displacement Devices create a distortion field that makes a ship appear to be in a different place than it really is. This gives all beam and ballistic weapons a flat 30% of missing, regardless of other considerations, and gives the ship a +25% to missile defense.

ECM Jammers generate electronic interference in a missile or torpedo guidance system that can often cause the warheads to go astray. Jammers add +75% to missile defense.

Energy Absorbers interpose fields of null space in front of oncoming beams, ballistic ordinance, and missile/torpedo warheads. The null space fields absorb about a quarter of the kinetic/radient energy of the incoming firepower and store it in the absorber. In practice, the device blocks 25% of all incoming damage (the damage blocking effect is applied after the effect of a damper field), and stores it. When it comes time for the ship to move next, all of this stored energy (damage) is released at a target within 48 hexes. The energy bolt is very wide angle, and automatically hits, and is rather cohesive (no range penalties). Hit location is determined normally.

Fast Missile Racks supply all of a ship's missile racks with an auxillary, preloaded backup rack. This allows the missile racks to fire twice in the combat turn. The missile racks then fire at their normal rate until a turn is spend without firing to allow the fast racks a chance to reload.

Hard Shields increase the width of a shield's energy field, and improve its integrety. They absorb 3 damage from every successful attack, even after the corresponding shield facing has fallen. They also allow a ship's shields to functin inside of a nebula.

Heavy Armor is a simple concept: the ship is given an extra layer of armored alloy that multiplies the number of available armor points by three and protects the ship from armor piercing weapons (although not the Achilles Targeting Unit, or an Emissions Guidance Missile).

High Energy Foci magnify and refocus all the beam weapons on the ship, increasing their damage by 50% of their base damage (i.e. a heavy beam weapon will still have its damage increased by only 50% the normal beam weapon's damage). This bonus does not apply to ballistic weapons, missiles, or torps.

Hyper-X Capacitors are energy storage batteries coupled with an advanced cooling system that allow all of a ship's beam weapons to fire twice as many times as normal. The ship's beam weapons may then fire normally until they go unused for a round. This recharges the capacitors, allowing the beams to fire twice again.

Inertial Nullifiers create a stable hyperspacial field around the ship, reducing its inertia almost to zero for manuvering purposes. This cuts the movement cost for changing facing to 0 and gives the ship a +100 to defense.

Inertial Stabilizers create a partial hyperspacial field around the ship, significantly reducing its inertia for manuvering purposes. This cuts the movement cost for changing facing to 2 MP per hexside change, and gives the ship a +50 to defense.

Lightning Fields surround the ship with a powerful ion field that has a straight 50% chance of frying the guidance systems of any incoming missiles, rupturing incoming torpedo containment systems, and destroying the electronics on incoming fighters.

Multi-Phased Shields generate several simultaneous deflector shields, each with a differing phase constant. Although the inevitability of destructive interference greatly reduces the strength of each shield, the total strength of all the phased shields added together is greater than any individual shield. The net result: the amount of damage a ship's shields can absorb (not the amount they can block) is increased by 50%.

Multi-Wave ECM Jammers are a significant improvement over the standard models, expanding their locking interference over most of the electromagnetic spectrum and also blocking neutrino and tachyon auxillery scanning devices. This gives the ship a +100% to missile defense, which is not cumulative with an ECM Jammer.

Phasing Cloaks are vastly improved Cloaking Devices that work by shifting a ship halfway between normal space and hyperspace. This makes the ship totally inaccessable to both dimensions, giving it complete immunity to all attacks, effects, and damage, while allowing it to move and scan its surroundings normally. The ship must uncloak to fire, but may cloak again on its next turn, provided it doesn't fire its weapon. A phasing cloak does, however, interfere with the neurological functions of the crew (it partially phases out their neutral pathways, after all). As a safety precaution, the phasing cloak steps down to a cloaking device after 10 turns of combat to prevent any perminant damage to the crew.

Quantum Detonators amplify the force of an engine explosion by three times, and are programmed to go off anytime a ship loses all of its structural integrety. They also have a 50% chance of detonating if the ship is succussfully captured. Although the computer controls for this device aren't complicated, the quantum amplifiers used in the detonator itself haven't been seen since the Orion Era. This means the detonators haven't been used since the Orion Era either.

Rangemaster Units are computer targeting systems specifically designed to compensate for a beam or ballistic weapon's range inaccuracies. A ship equipped with this system reduces effective range to 1/3 to determine the range penalty to hit only.

Reinforced Hulls are straightforward. The ship designer trades internal space for extra structural support, and shielding for the ship's internal systems. This modification triples the ship's SI value, and subtracts 4 from the ship's chances of taking a critical hit when its internal structure is hit.

Security Stations monitor vital areas of a military ship, and attack any intruders with automated weapons systems. This increases the combat strength of all defending marines by 20.

Shield Capacitors are powerful generators and battery systems dedicated to providing auxillary power to the ship's shields. This improves the shield regeneration rate from 30% of the maximum facing strength to 100%.

Structural Analyzers are scanners that search out weak spots in the ship's armor and structure. They double the damage of all beam and ballistic weapons that penetrate the shields.

Subspace Teleporters allow a ship to make highly localized combat jumps through hyperspace. Essentially, once each turn, the ship can teleport itself up to 18 hexes in any direction. It's facing remains the same. The teleporter does not improve ship defense.

Transporters are sophisticated matter-to-energy conversion systems that scan the molecular patterns of something, living or no, transform it into a targeted beam of photons, and then revert the photons back to normal matter, duplicating the stored pattern exactly. This allows the ship to transport raiding parties to enemy ship up to 12 hexes away, and bombs to a planet 12 hexes away. A transporter cannot beam through a deflector shield, however, so the shield facing the transporting ship must be down, and the ship can't have hard shields. Furthermore, a damper field seriously weakens the transporter beam, making it impossible to restore the transported person.

Troop Pods are like extra, on-ship barracks for marines. Troop Pods double the number of marines on board the ship.

Warp Dissipaters generate a powerful quantum stabilization field that totally prevents any enemy ships from generating a direct portal into hyperspace. Although this has no effect on intertial stabilizers and nullifiers, it does prevent sub space teleporters from working, and prevents ships from retreating in combat.

Wide Area Jammers generate a powerful electromagnetic, tachyonic, and neutrino interferance field that blankets the equipped ship and every other ship in the fleet. This gives the equipped ship a +130% bonus to missile defense, and every other ship a +75% missile defense. The +75% defense is cumulative with lesser jammers, but two Wide Area Jammers do not have a cumulative effect.
